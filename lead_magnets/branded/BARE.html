The Unbox
Eye-level, 50mm, Kodak Portra 160, soft directional light from the left, fine grain. Her hands mid-pull — lifting two amber glass bottles from a bone-colored box lined with sage linen, the tissue paper crinkling and half-torn, one sheet still draped over the remaining bottles inside like a curtain she hasn't opened yet. The box sitting on a terrazzo countertop, the "Bare" logo debossed into the lid in sage green, subtle enough to miss if you're not looking. Inside: four amber bottles nestled in linen dividers, each one catching the window light differently — the serum glowing golden, the toner darker, the cleanser catching a reflection of her wrist. A brass zipper pouch tucked in the corner holding samples. Tissue paper everywhere — one piece on the counter, one still in her grip, one on the floor where it drifted. Her hands real — short nails, a thin silver ring on her index finger, the tendons visible as she grips the bottle neck. The anticipation in the frame: she hasn't tried any of it yet, the caps still sealed, the labels unsmudged, everything still a promise. Behind her, soft focus: a marble bathroom shelf waiting for these bottles to find their places. Portra 160 pulling warm amber from the glass and cool sage from the linen. The first moment before the routine begins.

The Mask
Close-up, 85mm, Kodak Portra 160, soft warm bathroom light from a single overhead bulb and window ambient, fine grain. Her face covered in a thin layer of sage-tinted clay mask — the product drying at the edges near her hairline where it was applied first, still wet and glossy across her cheeks where it was applied last. Eyes closed, head tilted back slightly against the bathroom wall, the fifteen minutes of nothing. The clay cracking in hairline fractures along her smile lines, the texture like a dry riverbed in miniature. Her real skin visible at the mask edges — the transition from product to pore, the clay feathering into bare skin at her jawline where she didn't blend carefully. One earlobe showing, a small gold stud. Her lips bare, slightly dry, slightly parted. Behind her in soft focus: sage green bathroom tiles in a subway pattern, grout lines slightly darkened with age, a brass towel hook, the amber "Bare" mask jar on the edge of the tub with the lid off and a wooden spatula resting across it. A dog-eared paperback balanced on her knee — she's reading, or was, before she closed her eyes. The clay pulling her skin taut, the discomfort that means it's working. Portra 160 rendering the sage clay against warm skin tones, the grain sitting in the mask texture. Skincare as patience, not performance.

The Garden
Low angle from soil level, 35mm, Kodak Portra 160, early morning golden hour, dew still on everything, fine grain. Her hands in the dirt — fingers buried to the second knuckle in dark wet soil, pulling a chamomile plant from the earth, the root system trailing soil and fine white threads. The garden bed stretching behind her: rows of lavender, rosemary, calendula, all slightly overgrown and tangled in the way a working garden gets when production matters more than aesthetics. Morning dew on every leaf and petal — each droplet a tiny lens catching the low sun, the chamomile flowers heavy with water and nodding. Her hands real: soil under her short nails, a small scratch across her knuckle from yesterday, her skin slightly rough and reddened from the morning cold. A brass garden marker pushed into the soil reading "chamomile — batch 52" in handwritten script. Beside her: a woven basket already half-full with harvested herbs, a pair of brass scissors, a linen pouch with the "Bare" logo for transporting cuttings to the lab. The soil dark and rich, worm castings visible, the organic matter that becomes the product months from now. A weathered wooden raised bed edge, lichen growing on the south side. The sun backlighting the dew and the fine hairs on the plant stems, turning everything to amber and sage. Portra 160 at its warmest, the grain mixing with soil texture. The raw materials before they become anything.

The Hands
Overhead close-up, macro, Kodak Portra 160, hard flash mixed with soft window fill, fine grain, razor-sharp on the hands. Her hands mid-moisturize — fingers of the right hand pressing cream into the back of the left, the product thick and white, spreading in a slow wave across her knuckles, catching in the creases between her fingers where the skin folds. The cream texture visible: dense, not whipped, tiny air bubbles where it was scooped from the jar. Her skin real — the knuckle wrinkles deep and detailed, a hangnail on her ring finger, the faint blue of a vein crossing the back of her hand, dry cuticles that the cream is about to fix. On the terrazzo counter beside her: the ring she took off — a thin gold band sitting in a small puddle of light, the inside of the band slightly dulled from years of wearing. The "Bare" moisturizer jar open, the brass lid beside it upside down showing the embossed logo, the cream surface inside showing the divot where her fingers scooped. A cotton pad, unused. A single amber glass bottle catching flash, throwing a warm reflection across the stone. The terrazzo surface — sage and bone aggregate, cool to the touch, one water drop near the faucet base. The frame tight on her hands and the counter, nothing else. Portra 160 pulling every skin tone from pink knuckle to golden wrist, the flash making the cream glow white against warm skin. The whole routine in two hands.

The Morning
Wide shot, 28mm, Kodak Portra 160, soft morning window light flooding from a large frosted bathroom window on the right, fine grain. The full bathroom — she's mid-routine at the sink, not posing, not aware of the camera, caught in the ordinary. Her left hand on the brass faucet turning it off, her right holding a damp washcloth against her neck, water dripping from her jaw onto the counter. She's in a bone linen robe, untied, the collar open, her hair clipped up with a tortoiseshell claw clip, damp pieces falling around her ears. The bathroom counter sprawling across the frame: all five "Bare" amber bottles in various states of use — one cap off, one on its side from being knocked, the serum dropper out and resting on a folded tissue leaving a golden stain. A terrazzo countertop with water drops, a brass soap dish, a glass tumbler with a toothbrush. The sage green subway tile wall behind her reflecting the window light in soft matte tones. A brass-framed mirror catching her shoulder and the window behind. On the floor near the bath mat: a pair of slippers, kicked off at different angles. The window light creating long soft shadows across the tile floor, the room bright but not clinical, warm but not styled. Everything slightly messy, nothing arranged. Portra 160 rendering the bone robe, the amber glass, the sage tile, and her bare skin all in the same warm breath. The shot a real morning actually looks like when the skincare is good and nobody's watching.
